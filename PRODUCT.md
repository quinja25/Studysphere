# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

IB and A-Level students aged 15–19, studying under a structured and high-pressure curriculum. They use StudySphere in focused study sessions — not passively browsing. Their context: late evenings before exams, working through past papers, stuck on a concept. Secondary users are alumni and mentors who contribute answers and resources.

The student's core job: get an accurate, curriculum-scoped answer fast — without wading through irrelevant content from generic search or ChatGPT. They trust specificity over breadth.

## Product Purpose

An AI-powered study platform scoped to IB and A-Level curriculum. The core product is a RAG-based AI tutor that cites real sources: past papers, textbooks, community answers, uploaded notes. Layered on top: community Q&A, Pomodoro study rooms with video, and a resources marketplace.

Primary differentiator: the AI knows the curriculum. It doesn't hallucinate off-topic answers — it retrieves from a curated knowledge base and references the student's own documents. Success looks like a student who gets a better grade because they studied with StudySphere.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, focused, confident. StudySphere respects the student's intelligence and their time pressure. The voice is direct — no filler, no cheerleading, no "Great question!" The interface feels like a well-calibrated tool, not a study app competing for attention with TikTok.

Reference: Linear / Raycast — precision-crafted, every element earns its place, treats the user as capable.

Emotional goal: confidence and calm. The student should feel more in control after using it, not more anxious.

## Anti-references

- **Generic edtech** (Quizlet, Khan Academy): bright primary colors, quiz-game aesthetics, progress bars and confetti that feel juvenile. StudySphere does not look like a flashcard app.
- **AI product clichés**: purple gradients fading to black, floating neural-net orbs, "Ask me anything" hero chatboxes, glowing neon accents, glassmorphism panels. The AI capability is real — it doesn't need to announce itself with visual theatrics.

## Design Principles

1. **Curriculum gravity** — visual decisions should reinforce that this tool is scoped, expert, and specific. Generic = untrustworthy.
2. **Pressure absorption** — students are already stressed. Every layout, spacing, and color decision should reduce cognitive load, not add to it.
3. **Earn the space** — no decorative elements. If something doesn't carry information or guide action, it doesn't exist.
4. **Tool-grade precision** — UI components should feel as considered as Linear's issue list or Raycast's command bar. Consistent, tight, intentional.
5. **Source-first credibility** — citations, chunk references, and provenance are first-class UI elements. They're the product's proof of honesty.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Best-effort accessible for now. Target WCAG AA contrast ratios for text. Support `prefers-reduced-motion`. No color-only information. Tighten requirements once user research identifies specific needs.
